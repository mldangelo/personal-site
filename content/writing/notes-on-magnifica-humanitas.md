date: '2026-05-25'

_Disclosure: I work at OpenAI. The opinions in this piece are my own._

Pope Leo XIV published his first encyclical on May 25, 2026. He chose to write about artificial intelligence.

The document was signed ten days earlier, on the 135th anniversary of [_Rerum Novarum_](https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiii/en/encyclicals/documents/hf_l-xiii_enc_15051891_rerum-novarum.html), Leo XIII's 1891 encyclical on industrial capitalism, private property, and worker dignity. Leo places AI in that lineage: a transformation of labor, property, power, and human dignity.

[_Magnifica Humanitas_](https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html) reads AI through the older language of Catholic social teaching: labor, property, truth, war, education, human dignity, and the common good. It presents AI as a social technology, one that can reshape work, schools, media, military doctrine, and the terms on which people participate in public life. Model behavior is only one layer of the problem Leo describes.

The Vatican operates on different terrain from a legislature, cloud provider, military customer, or frontier lab. It names categories, convenes actors, and makes certain claims about technology harder to describe as neutral. From outside the Church, the document can look almost like a policy intervention. From inside it, the claim is stronger: AI has become a matter for social doctrine.

At the May 25 presentation, [Vatican News listed Christopher Olah](https://www.vaticannews.va/en/pope/news/2026-05/pope-leo-xiv-first-encyclical-magnifica-humanitas.html), an Anthropic co-founder and interpretability researcher, among the speakers. Olah's presence put a major AI lab beside a document warning about the private concentration of technological power.

A cynical reading is that Anthropic gained proximity to Vatican moral authority. Anthropic also stood inside the critique.

Olah works on technical opacity: what advanced AI systems are doing internally. Leo's encyclical asks a social version of the same question. What institutions are we building around systems their builders only partly understand?

One premise runs through the document: tools carry the priorities of the institutions that design, fund, regulate, and deploy them. A hiring model is personnel policy. A tutoring model is education policy. A military decision-support system becomes part of the command structure. A model used to summarize the news becomes part of the public sphere.

The encyclical's controlling questions are institutional. Who defines correctness? Who controls infrastructure? Who benefits? Who bears the cost? Who can appeal? Labs have to answer some of those questions. So do lawmakers, procurement officials, schools, professional bodies, and democratic institutions.

The document's most direct challenge to AI companies may be its treatment of property. Catholic social teaching recognizes private property within a broader claim about the goods owed to all. Leo extends the "universal destination of goods" to "patents, algorithms, digital platforms, technological infrastructure and data." That is more pointed than a generic call for responsible AI. The basic materials of the digital economy increasingly determine who can work, learn, speak, and participate; Leo treats their concentration as a moral question.

That claim makes _Magnifica Humanitas_ more radical than much corporate AI ethics language. The document asks companies to be careful and then presses further: the concentration of data, compute, platforms, and technical knowledge may be a moral problem in itself.

Pope Francis had already made AI part of the Vatican's public agenda. In 2024, he called for a [binding international treaty](https://www.vatican.va/content/francesco/en/messages/peace/documents/20231208-messaggio-57giornatamondiale-pace2024.html), warned about ["cognitive pollution"](https://www.vatican.va/content/francesco/en/messages/communications/documents/20240124-messaggio-comunicazioni-sociali.html), and told the [G7](https://www.vatican.va/content/francesco/en/speeches/2024/june/documents/20240614-g7-intelligenza-artificiale.html) that machines may choose; only people decide. The 2025 Vatican note [_Antiqua et Nova_](https://press.vatican.va/content/salastampa/en/bollettino/pubblico/2025/01/28/250128b.html) gave the theological premise: AI can imitate some products of intelligence without becoming human intelligence.

Leo gathers those arguments into Catholic social teaching. He is trying to decide what kind of problem AI is. His answer begins with work, property, truth, violence, and the human person.

The labor argument is the clearest link to _Rerum Novarum_. Leo accepts one promise of automation: technology can relieve people of mundane or repetitive work. Efficiency alone supplies no justification for large-scale displacement, deskilling, worker surveillance, or the transfer of adaptation costs onto people with little bargaining power.

Work gives people structure, contribution, competence, social standing, and a way of belonging to a community. A technology that changes work at scale changes more than the economy.

Much AI commentary treats automation as either simple abundance or simple harm. The unresolved question is institutional: if AI weakens the link between work and livelihood, what distributes the gains, protects displaced workers, and preserves human agency? The encyclical offers no labor program. Its contribution is to keep the question from being buried under inevitability.

The same logic applies to the hidden labor behind AI. The public imagination tends to see models, products, and demos. The actual system also includes data labeling, content moderation, energy infrastructure, chips, minerals, and supply chains. A debate framed only around future unemployment misses the work already being absorbed, displaced, or made invisible.

The sections on truth and education deserve the same attention. Leo treats truth as a common good, larger than the narrower problem of content moderation. AI can amplify falsehoods. The deeper risk is a public sphere in which verification, shared attention, and trust become harder to sustain. His proposed counterweight is practical: schools, families, and institutions that teach reading, silence, discernment, and the slow work of judgment.

That argument may sound old-fashioned. It is also practical. More fluent machines offer no rescue to a society that loses the capacity to judge what is true.

The war section applies the same concern to coercive power. Francis said at the G7 that no machine should ever choose to take a human life. Leo extends that warning into a broader argument about the automation and normalization of violence. Even without sentience, AI can change war by making targeting faster, accountability murkier, and lethal decisions feel more procedural.

The Church's language is absolute; the practical concern is plain. Some decisions get worse when they become frictionless. Human judgment matters because it can be accountable, embodied, slow, and morally exposed.

That cuts against a central product instinct in AI: reduce friction wherever possible. Leo asks which frictions protect the person.

His critique of transhumanism is the most theological part of the encyclical. Even in secular terms, the argument is clear. The document pushes back against visions of progress that treat limitation, dependence, aging, and vulnerability as defects to be corrected. Medicine, accessibility tools, and technologies that reduce suffering fit comfortably inside this view. The target is a harsher assumption: the dependent person as an inefficient version of the autonomous one.

A society that treats every limit as an optimization target will eventually treat some people as obsolete.

Religious and religion-linked institutions are reaching for similar language. In 2024, religious leaders from 11 traditions signed the Rome Call for AI Ethics in [Hiroshima](https://www.wcrp.or.jp/en/ai_ethics_for_peace). [ICESCO](https://icesco.org/en/2024/09/12/at-the-global-ai-summit-icesco-director-general-announces-the-launch-of-riyadh-ai-ethics-charter-for-the-islamic-world/) and Saudi Arabia's data authority launched an AI ethics charter for the Islamic world. Egypt's [Dar al-Ifta](https://www.dar-alifta.org/en/fatwa/details/22255/using-ai-applications-to-obtain-fatwas) has warned against relying on AI to obtain fatwas. The [Church of England](https://www.churchofengland.org/media/press-releases/synod-affirms-work-key-human-dignity-and-purpose-face-ai-revolution) has debated work and human dignity under automation.

Some of these efforts will matter more than others. Together, they show that AI has escaped the technology beat.

Religious institutions have no special claim to settle AI policy. The Church is also an awkward messenger on opaque power. Its own history includes abuses of authority and delayed moral clarity on questions it now treats as obvious. The argument still deserves attention, and it raises the standard: critiques of unaccountable power should apply to churches, AI labs, governments, and markets alike.

Technologists should notice what these religious responses reveal. As AI systems are used or proposed for tutoring children, screening applicants, summarizing news, assisting doctors, supporting military decisions, and mediating access to public services, they become part of the machinery through which people learn, work, decide, and seek help.

Professional ethics has long said much of this. The [ACM](https://www.acm.org/code-of-ethics) and [IEEE](https://www.ieee.org/about/corporate/governance/p7-8.html) have long told computing professionals and engineers to put the public interest first. The gap is enforcement, incentives, and professional seriousness. Software borrowed the prestige of engineering without adopting many of engineering's constraints.

Better models leave the hardest questions unanswered: who gets displaced, who gets profiled, who can appeal, and who absorbs the cost. The common good has to be argued over, protected by law, reflected in procurement, enforced in workplaces, debated in schools, and tested in public institutions.

Secular readers can reject many premises of _Magnifica Humanitas_ and still take its central question seriously. What kinds of human lives are we making easier to live, and what kinds are we making harder?

The answer has to be worked out among labs, regulators, churches, legislatures, workers, schools, and the public that will live with the result.
